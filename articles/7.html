Safety and Supervision: Balancing Independence with Security in Youth Spaces

Written by Maxton Marshall

First, a safe youth space considers safety without jeopardizing the independence and independent want of teens. Teens want independence to socialize, roam, and make decisions. However, parents want a guaranteed safe place for their teens, so the successful youth space employs safety measures that are hidden, if you will, through sight lines, trained staff, and anticipated standards. These exist for safety, without existing for safety as if the adults are always there watching. Thus, safety needs to support independence instead of supplanting it.

In addition, there should be designs incorporated that allow teens to support their own sense of responsibility to make them feel more feel more independent within the space. For example, if teens create the guidelines and standards, then through the process of engaging in peer leadership, a sense of ownership is fostered that results in respect and acknowledgment of the space. If the kids help create the expectations, they will be more likely to honor them and feel empowered rather than controlled. Thus, this represents how adults trust in teens supports the relationship. Ultimately, these spaces empower youth because where safety and independence complicate each other, creative and strategic planning makes it so that a space can exist that is safe but offers the freedom proper standards would otherwise lack. When a space provides freedom with the proper safety elements, youth feel respected and welcomed within them.
