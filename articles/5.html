The Digital Playground: Integrating Technology and Physical Spaces to Meet Modern Youth Needs

Written by Cameron Mosier

The Role of Transit and Walkability in Youth Accessibility

The world is changing.

The physical and digital worlds are increasingly overlapping, converging into what experts call "phygital" spaces where technology and real-world experiences are inextricably linked. This shift has fostered a "mobile-first" and digital-first approach to interacting with the world, a trend that public institutions like libraries have effectively adapted to, particularly following the Covid-19 pandemic.

Data illustrates this massive digital shift in library usage: patrons and students checked out a combined 326 million eBooks, audiobooks, and digital magazines globally between 2018 and 2019. (*11) This number then surged to over 430 million digital items borrowed in 2020 alone, demonstrating libraries' successful integration into a digitally native world. (*10) However, one of the main reasons for this surge was Covid-19.

Although pandemic restrictions have lifted, we are still using the digital world more than ever. Data from the Pew Research Center shows that 48% of Americans say the COVID-19 pandemic changed the way they use technology. This includes 18% who say the pandemic changed their current technology use in a major way. (*12)

Bridging the Gap: The Evolution of Traditional Spaces

While digital access is crucial, a physical space still provides a unique, hands-on learning environment that purely digital libraries cannot replicate.. This is the reason why many libraries are introducing public makerspaces.

In recent years, many libraries have incorporated computers into their spaces. Now, they are making makerspaces that include 3D printers, sewing machines, and laser cutters to video/audio gear, art supplies, and digital conversion tools (VHS to digital), plus lots more. (*13)

But many of these makerspaces include coding workshops. According to americanlibrariesmagazine.org, more than one in five libraries provide classes or informal help related to coding, computer programming, robotics, and 3D printing. (*14)

While 3D printers (offered by 20% of libraries), virtual reality headsets (13%), and smart boards (7%) are the “new kids on the block,” libraries are often among the few public places that still maintain ready access to 'older' technology needed by many people, such as copy machines, printers, and fax machines. (*14)
