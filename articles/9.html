Youth-Led Design: The Power of Letting Teens Shape Their Own Environments

Written by Maxton Marshall

Youth-led design provides teens with an opportunity to impact the spaces they use daily, and such psychological involvement and affect can go a long way in benefiting the population. For example, participatory design research states that when teens are involved in suggesting what goes into their parks, community centers, or public places of gathering, they feel appreciated. Research on participatory design also indicates that spaces that young people help develop and grow their bond with the space and likelihood of use and stewardship.

In cities across the nation, youth design councils Have been developed to allow teens to suggest skate parks, lounges for studying, or art walls. In this case study, it's clear that teens are more aware of what other youths desire than adults are. In turn, spaces created from youth involvement become more useful, relevant, and engaging; when you feel a sense of ownership over the design, they're more likely to honor the space and encourage others to do so to engage as well.

Finally, youth public involvement increases equity in many environments. Youth needs are often overlooked and avoided by many communities; however, despite public places being used by many more genes than anticipated, young people fail to receive consideration as a critical stakeholder. By involving them in participatory processes, designers ensure that the space ultimately reflects the relevant user experience. Thus, youth led design creates a strong group community fostering confidence, belongingness, and trust while developing spaces teams really want to use.
