AI in the Youth Center: Enhancing Education, Creativity, and Career Prep

Written by Cameron Mosier

Current Landscape: Students and Teachers in the Hub

Youth spaces are becoming primary locations where students access technology outside of home and school. The adoption of AI tools by teenagers for academic purposes has risen rapidly. A Pew Research Center survey found that the share of teens using ChatGPT specifically for schoolwork more than doubled, increasing to 26% in 2024 from 13% in 2023. This usage often happens in public settings like libraries, making these spaces crucial for providing support. (*3)

For educators and mentors running programs within these hubs, AI offers significant time-saving benefits for administrative tasks. Nearly 60% of teachers are using AI for tasks such as grading and lesson planning. Teachers who use AI weekly report saving almost 6 hours per week. (*2)

The Ethical Dilemma: Policies, Plagiarism, and Privacy in Safe Spaces

The structured environment of a youth space or library is ideal for navigating the ethical dilemmas presented by AI. The rapid adoption of tools like ChatGPT has redefined traditional definitions of plagiarism and academic integrity, a conversation best moderated by mentors in a physical location.

As previously mentioned, nearly 60% of teachers are using AI for tasks like grading and lesson planning, often saving significant amounts of time. If teachers can use it, why should the students not be able to use it?

The main reason teachers are permitted to use AI is that they possess the foundational knowledge to complete the tasks themselves, even without the tool. For students, it's a different scenario; using AI can be compared to handing someone a calculator before they learn basic mental math. Yes, they may get the correct answer, but this approach bypasses the essential learning process, which could lead to failure later in life. (*4, *5)

One significant ethical and legal concern is student data privacy. When students interact with commercial AI platforms, they may input sensitive information. The core issue is that many third-party AI models use user inputs for training their next generations of technology, raising red flags regarding data governance.

Physical youth centers are uniquely positioned to address these issues. By providing supervised computer labs and pre-vetted software, they can ensure compliance with regulations such as the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) and the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA). These laws protect student data privacy and require parental consent for data collection from children under 13. Youth spaces can implement clear data-sharing agreements with vendors that explicitly prohibit the use of student data for AI model training, offering a layer of safety often missing from home use. (*6)

Future Proofing: AI Literacy in the Job Market within Dedicated Labs

In the future job market, AI literacy will be an essential skill. Youth centers and library Maker Spaces can serve as critical preparation grounds. By offering workshops and dedicated "AI Labs," these spaces can help teens develop the essential skills that complement AI rather than compete with it: critical thinking, problem-solving, and adaptability. These physical hubs provide the necessary infrastructure and expert guidance for teens to experiment with AI safely and ethically, ensuring they are prepared for future careers.

Within these dedicated AI Labs, teens can move beyond basic querying and engage in practical applications. They can participate in workshops focused on "AI-assisted creativity," using image generators like DALL-E or Midjourney to produce digital art, thereby developing visual literacy and prompt engineering skills. Other sessions could focus on using AI for data analysis for school science projects or leveraging AI coding assistants to debug their first lines of code. This hands-on experience, guided by mentors, bridges the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical application, ensuring skills are built in a supervised environment.

Crucially, these labs are not just about using technology; they are about developing the unique human skills that complement AI rather than compete with it. The facilitated discussions within these spaces foster critical thinking, media literacy, and ethical reasoning. Ultimately, the impact of AI on youth will not be determined by school bans, but by their ability to adapt and learn how to use these powerful tools responsibly. By developing a strong ethical compass and embracing AI literacy now within supportive community hubs, teenagers can navigate the transition from classroom to career with confidence. The future job market is ready for AI; the question is, are you ready to embrace the challenge within your own community?
