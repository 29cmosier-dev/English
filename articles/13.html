Designed for Engagement: The Features That Make a Youth Space Successful

Written by Cameron Mosier

Some of the most important questions asked when building a community center for teens is, “How it will be built?”, “What will be in it?” and “How expensive it will be to build a community center for teenagers?”

Research into existing youth spaces indicates that most designs are either sleek and modern, with neon colors, or old-fashioned and wooden. Many of these rooms use geometric shapes, whether on the floor or the walls.

Moving away from furniture, many of those youth rooms had games, with some of the most popular being foosball, air hockey, and ping-pong. This brings back the arcade from the 1980’s, but with a modern touch. Lighting was another important factor in these designs, with all kinds of light fixtures and brightness. Across all successful designs, several key elements consistently emerged as crucial for fostering engagement:

  • Comfortable Seating

    Teens often seek relaxed, non-traditional seating options, which is a key design element for fostering a welcoming environment. The inclusion of varied seating, such as couches, beanbags, and modular furniture, encourages socializing and a sense of ownership, moving beyond rigid, institutional seating.

  • Reliable Wi-Fi

    In the digital age, internet access is not a luxury but a necessity for most young people. Providing high-speed, reliable Wi-Fi supports both educational activities (homework, research) and social engagement (streaming, connecting with peers).

  • Food Options

    Access to snacks and drinks makes a space significantly more inviting, especially for teens who might spend extended periods there after school. This can range from simple amenities like vending machines to a small cafe area or a designated, clean space for users to bring their own food.

  • Multi-functional Areas

    Flexibility is key when designing spaces to accommodate the diverse and evolving interests of young people. A successful design incorporates areas that can be easily transformed to suit different needs, such as a large open room for events that can be partitioned for quieter study zones or small group activities.

  • Involving Youth in the Design Process (Placemaking)

    The most critical element for a project's long-term success is involving the target audience directly in the design process. Through placemaking initiatives, youth can provide meaningful input on everything from colors and furniture to technology and programming, ensuring the final space truly reflects their needs and desires. (*7)

Ultimately, the success of a youth space isn't determined by a single feature like a beanbag chair or a ping-pong table. It emerges from a holistic approach to design that prioritizes the user experience above all else. By integrating comfortable, flexible furniture, essential technology like reliable Wi-Fi, basic amenities such as food options, and creating highly adaptable multi-functional zones, designers can build the physical infrastructure for engagement. However, the most vital takeaway is that these spaces only truly thrive when the young people themselves are given a seat at the design table. True placemaking ensures the space is not just 'for' teens, but genuinely 'theirs,' fostering ownership and guaranteeing long-term community success.
