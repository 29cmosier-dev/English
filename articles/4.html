The Commute Factor: Ensuring Youth Spaces are Accessible via Public Transit and Active Transport

Written by Maxton Marshall

The Role of Transit and Walkability in Youth Accessibility

Available youth-spaces require good transit and citizen-friendly design and safety. When destinations are located near reliable bus stops, bike paths, and safe sidewalks, more youths/teens can locate them, especially teens without cars. Research shows that teens/youth are more likely to walk, bike, or use public transport to get to school, their job, or community sites when the built environment supports active and transit access. (*8, *9)

Yet proximity to transit further complicates equity of access for community programming. Many teens walk, bike or ride the bus; youth-spaces not located near transit lines effectively deny many of those who would benefit the most from access to such places. Therefore, social equity, opportunity, and siting connect. If a teen cannot safely access a center, they forfeit opportunities that facilitate support, recreation and socialization.

Therefore, the spaces should be constructed where all community teens – without access to a car – can access them. Safe sidewalks and protected bike lanes and established public transit routes mean access for all teens, not just those who can drive. Ultimately, transit access promotes youth engagement, empowerment and enhanced quality of life.
